Research Overview
Objective & Scope
What this document covers and how it was built.

Understand which onboarding mechanics work best to help PayWise users reach their first transaction within 48 hours of registration. This brief expands the Genesis Report's Activation Gap findings into concrete, T&T-specific recommendations for new users and existing users who have registered but not transacted recently.

Source Synthesis

This summary synthesises the Genesis Report, the PayWise knowledge base, the Buyer Psychology Report, the Master Strategy Document, the Channel Acquisition Map, and external fintech onboarding research. Recommendations are shaped for a product operating in Trinidad & Tobago's trust-first, cash-heavy market. Where a recommendation depends on infrastructure not yet in place (push notifications, SMS), that dependency is flagged explicitly.

The Aha Moment
Specific Per User Type
The first transaction goal is not generic — defining a precise aha moment per user type matters more than covering all possible first actions equally.

Getting a user to one specific, high-value first action within 48 hours is what predicts retention — not exposure to the full feature set. The strongest aha moment candidates share three qualities: they are non-discretionary (the need exists independently of PayWise), they produce an immediate tangible outcome the user can see and feel, and they create a recurring loop — a reason to return next month without being prompted.

User Type Their Aha Moment Why It Works
Everyday Consumer First bill paid — receipt confirmed Non-discretionary, monthly, immediate proof the app works
Small Business Owner First card payment received Solves the pain that drove signup; visible revenue impact
Bill Payment Agent First customer transaction processed + commission visible Proves the income model is real before the deposit feels risky
Diaspora Sender Family confirms receipt of transfer Emotional stakes are highest here — success = deep trust earned
E-Commerce Merchant First checkout completed via PayWise Validates integration and ends dual-platform complexity
Practical Implication Onboarding should not route all new users through the same first action. The product should detect or ask which track the user is on at signup — consumer, business, or agent — and surface the aha moment relevant to that track immediately. A business owner being prompted to pay a utility bill first is a mismatch that will cost the activation.

For the consumer track specifically — which is the largest segment and the one most directly addressed by the 48-hour activation window — the first successful bill payment remains the strongest aha moment and the primary recommendation throughout the new user and lapsed user sections below. All other tracks should apply the same progressive disclosure logic to their own equivalent first action.

03
New User Onboarding
Problems & Recommendations
Five friction points blocking first-time users from completing a bill payment within 48 hours of signup.
Problem 1

Users register but do not immediately understand the next step

📊 Evidence

PayWise's current home screen surfaces approximately 14 options simultaneously — Cash Out, TopUp Wallet, Bank Accounts, Mobile TopUp, QR Codes, Pay Bill, Agent Locations, Gift Cards, ePOS, Refer Friends, and more. Fintech onboarding research consistently shows that users convert better when guided to a single first-value action. Users presented with this many choices will, statistically, choose nothing.

✅ Recommendation

Apply progressive disclosure, not a checklist. The new user home screen should surface exactly one action — the one most relevant to the track the user selected at signup. For consumers: "Pay a bill — 30 seconds." For business owners: "Accept your first payment." For agents: "Process your first customer bill." All other features become visible only after that first action is completed.

A progress checklist still shows multiple items. Progressive disclosure shows one. The distinction matters because any list of options — even a short one — reintroduces choice paralysis.

Problem 2

Trust barriers slow down or prevent the first payment

📊 Evidence

Trust is the first filter every buyer type applies — ahead of price, convenience, and features. In T&T, the 30+ demographic carries a structurally different trust barrier rooted in decades of institutional disappointment and visible scam exposure in tight-knit communities.

The CBTT license is PayWise's single most powerful trust asset and is currently underused. It answers the question: "Is this regulated, or is this a random app?"

✅ Recommendation

For all users: The CBTT license credential must appear on the first onboarding screen, before any feature or action prompt. Not in the footer. Not on screen three. Screen one. Approved phrase: "Licensed by the Central Bank of Trinidad and Tobago."

For the 30+ segment specifically: Lead with regulation and accountability: "Licensed. Local. Regulated the same way your bank is." Convenience language comes after trust is established, not before.

Problem 3

Users do not get a strong reason to transact within 48 hours

📊 Evidence

Most users sign up because they were referred, saw an ad, or were curious — but they do not have an immediate, urgent reason to transact. Without a compelling trigger connected to a real-world event, the app sits idle. In T&T, the natural recurring triggers are utility bill cycles (T&TEC, WASA, Flow, Digicel, bMobile) and mobile top-up needs — both of which are monthly, predictable, and emotionally weighted (no one wants their lights cut).

✅ Recommendation

Add a bill-due reminder workflow that connects onboarding directly to real local payment cycles. If a user signs up near a bill due date or within a known top-up cycle window, immediately surface that use case as the first suggested action.

Message: "Your T&TEC is probably due this month. Pay it in 30 seconds right here."

⚠
Infrastructure Dependency

This recommendation requires active push notification and SMS capability. These are not yet live in the product. Enabling lifecycle messaging is a prerequisite for this mechanic. This should be treated as Priority 1 infrastructure work before any activation campaign scales.

Problem 4

Users have no immediate reward for completing the first transaction

📊 Evidence

Fintech activation research shows that incentives tied to completed actions outperform incentives tied to signup alone. A reward for downloading the app has no activation effect. A reward for completing the first bill payment converts the decision from "should I bother?" to "I get something for doing what I was going to do anyway."

✅ Recommendation

Introduce a first-transaction reward — $5 TTD wallet credit or fee relief — granted automatically upon completing the first bill payment within 48 hours of signup.

The reward must be: (1) immediate — visible before they reach the payment screen; (2) certain — no conditions requiring reading; (3) relevant — wallet credit, not a prize draw.

Problem 5

Users need trust reinforced at the moment of payment, not just at signup

📊 Evidence

Trust must be re-confirmed at each stage — in the ad, on the landing page, during onboarding, and at the first transaction screen. Trust established at signup does not carry automatically into the payment moment. The payment screen is the highest-anxiety moment in the flow: the user is about to move real money.

✅ Recommendation

Place the CBTT license signal and the support contact (WhatsApp: 868-723-8394) on the payment confirmation screen — not just in account settings or a help section.

Text at the confirmation step: "Your payment is protected. PayWise is licensed by the Central Bank of T&T."

04
Existing / Lapsed Users
Re-Engagement Strategy
PayWise registrants who have not transacted, or who transacted previously but have gone quiet.
Problem 1

Lapsed users have lost the habit loop

📊 Evidence

Habit formation research shows that people re-engage when the product becomes tied to a repeatable routine rather than a one-time experiment. A lapsed user who paid their Flow bill once and then stopped has not formed a habit — they completed a single action. The reactivation trigger must reconnect them to the next natural payment moment, not ask them to revisit the product generically.

✅ Recommendation

Reframe all reactivation messaging around the user's upcoming recurring need. "Your next bill is probably due" outperforms "We miss you — come back to PayWise."

Route returning users directly into the bill payment flow, not to the home screen. Do not restart them from zero.

Problem 2

WhatsApp is the primary re-engagement channel — not push, not email

📊 Evidence

For the core T&T bill-payer audience, WhatsApp seeding is a primary acquisition channel. In T&T's social fabric, a WhatsApp message from a known contact carries significantly more weight than an app notification or email. Word-of-mouth through WhatsApp groups is the highest-trust mechanism available in this market.

✅ Recommendation

For lapsed-user reactivation, WhatsApp should be the first channel deployed — ahead of push notifications and email.

Message format: "Your T&TEC is probably due this month — pay it in under a minute on PayWise." This is not a promotional message. It is a useful reminder sent at the moment of real relevance.

⚠
Infrastructure Dependency

Personalised WhatsApp outreach at scale requires a CRM connection or broadcast tool. In the interim, manual or semi-automated WhatsApp messages to identifiable lapsed segments (registered but zero transactions) can achieve the same result with lower tooling overhead.

Problem 3

Lapsed users may still carry the original trust fears from signup

📊 Evidence

Trust is not durable. A user who registered months ago and never transacted did not have a bad experience — they simply never had a good one. The trust present at signup has had no positive transaction to reinforce it and has likely faded or been displaced by competing narratives (scam stories in WhatsApp groups, negative experiences with other platforms).

✅ Recommendation

Reintroduce the CBTT license, the support contact, and a brief proof of safety whenever a dormant user receives a reactivation message. Do not assume existing familiarity.

The re-engagement message should behave like a first impression, not a follow-up.

Problem 4

Lapsed users need the lowest possible re-entry friction

📊 Evidence

Return-user activation research consistently shows that shorter paths produce higher completion rates. The goal is to reduce the number of decisions a returning user must make before they reach the payment action. Sending them to the home screen and asking them to navigate from scratch will lose them again.

✅ Recommendation

Deep-link all reactivation messages directly into the bill payment flow for the biller most relevant to that user (based on prior behaviour, or T&TEC as the default).

The user opens the message, taps the link, and is already on the payment screen. One decision: confirm and pay.

Referral Programme
Timing Matters
A v1 correction — when to trigger the referral prompt changes everything.
v1 Correction The investigation v1 recommended a dual-sided referral reward tied to the new user's first transaction — and that structure remains correct. However, the timing of the prompt for the referrer requires a specific correction from v1.
The Fixed Rule

The referral prompt should be triggered after the referrer's third successful transaction — not after signup and not after the first transaction.

Why the Timing Change Matters in T&T

Trust in this market is community-validated. A person who has used PayWise once cannot credibly vouch for it in a WhatsApp group or to a neighbour — they haven't formed genuine trust yet. A person who has paid three bills and received confirmation each time has a real experience to share. When they say "this works," it carries weight.

Triggering the referral too early produces low-quality shares that fail to convert, because the referred person's first question will be "do you actually use it?" — and the honest answer at transaction #1 is "not really yet."

Referrer

Trigger: After transaction #3

In-app referral prompt shown after the third successful bill payment. Wallet credit granted after the invited user completes their first bill payment.

Invited User (New)

Reward: First-payment incentive

Reduced fee or wallet credit on their first bill payment. Both rewards are immediate, visible, and tied to real product use — not signup or download.

Agent Track
Agent Onboarding — Scoping Note
A distinct track with its own friction points, outside the 48-hour consumer activation framework.
Scope Note The original investigation scoped silently to consumer and merchant users. Agent onboarding is a distinct track with its own friction points. The Master Strategy Document identifies agent churn at 60 days as a named risk. The key friction points are documented below.
Friction Point 1

The $2,500 TTD minimum deposit

Creates a psychological commitment barrier before any earnings are visible. The fix is a clear break-even calculator shown before the deposit is requested. At 30 transactions/day, the deposit is recovered in approximately 6 weeks.

Friction Point 2

T&TEC and WASA zero commission

Must be disclosed in the first agent communication, not discovered later. Hidden discovery is a trust-destruction event in a market where word travels fast. Frame as foot traffic drivers, not commission generators.

Friction Point 3

System reliability at peak periods

End-of-month bill rush is the agent's existential fear. The onboarding pack must include a written failure-resolution protocol before the agent goes live — not as a support fallback, but as a pre-onboarding commitment.

Recommended Next Step These friction points are not covered by the 48-hour activation framework — agents have a longer, more deliberate evaluation cycle (1–3 weeks). They should be addressed in a parallel agent onboarding document that includes: break-even calculator, T&TEC/WASA disclosure language, and a formal written failure-resolution protocol.

Caribbean Context
T&T / Caribbean Adaptation Notes
US and European fintech playbooks require specific adaptation before applying to T&T. Five differences that change the strategy.

Recommendations from US and European fintech playbooks require adaptation before applying to T&T. The specific differences to account for:

Difference 01

Trust is community-validated, not brand-validated

A WhatsApp recommendation from a known person outweighs any ad. Activation strategies must account for the community trust layer — this is not a nice-to-have; it is the primary mechanism by which trust transfers in T&T.

Difference 02

The 30+ demographic requires authority-first messaging

Speed and convenience language triggers skepticism in this group, not conversion. Lead with regulation and accountability. Convenience language comes after trust is established — never before.

Difference 03

Bill payment is the recurring anchor, not P2P

Unlike markets where peer-to-peer payment drives adoption, T&T's highest-frequency digital payment need is utility bills. Onboarding should be oriented around this specific use case — not around sending money to friends.

Difference 04

Cash feels safe; digital feels risky

The product must make the first digital transaction feel as safe as handing over physical cash to a person in a shop. That requires visible authority signals and a human support contact at every friction point in the flow.

Difference 05

WhatsApp is the communications infrastructure

In T&T, WhatsApp is where trust travels, where recommendations happen, and where bad stories spread. All activation and re-engagement strategies should treat it as the primary channel — not a secondary supplement to email or push.

The average person over the age of 30 in Trinidad and Tobago has a general lack of trust for technology… they still rather use traditional cash-based methods. Even if they know about the benefits of fintech… many fall into the habit of 'old talk' and are conservative rather than progressive.

Dual-Sided Incentive Programme
PayWise Local
Drawing from MercadoPago's QR adoption playbook in Argentina — adapted for T&T's community-trust market structure.

The Concept

When MercadoPago launched QR code payments in Argentina, they offered zero transaction costs for any payments made via QR for a limited period and invested heavily in consumer discounts to drive adoption. Their VP described the logic plainly: "The idea is to create an ecosystem where sellers and brands can subsidise these discounts in the future. But at the beginning, the effort was done a hundred percent on our side."

The result: 8.5 million QR payments processed in Argentina alone within the first year. The platform absorbed the cost early to build the habit. The ecosystem then sustained itself.

PayWise is in the same structural position — a market where cash dominates, digital payments are available but unfamiliar, and trust is community-validated rather than brand-built. A dual-sided incentive programme targets both the vendor and the consumer simultaneously, making each side the activation trigger for the other.

The Core Insight

In T&T's trust-first market, a neighbour paying for their groceries with their phone at the corner shop — and the shopkeeper accepting it without friction — is the most powerful product demonstration PayWise can produce. The people behind them in line have just received a trust signal no ad can replicate.

How It Works

For Local Vendors
  • Zero transaction fees on QR payments for the first 3 months after signup. The vendor takes on no cost while forming the habit of accepting digital payments.
  • A physical "PayWise Accepted Here" window sticker included in the signup pack. In T&T's cash-first environment, a familiar sticker on a trusted local store normalises digital payment in a way no ad can replicate.
  • A foot traffic bonus: vendors who onboard a minimum number of new PayWise users through their store receive a commission bonus on top of standard transaction earnings. This turns the vendor into an active advocate.
For New Consumers
  • A wallet credit ($10–15 TTD) redeemable only at participating local vendors — not for bill payments, not online. This forces the first PayWise transaction to happen at a physical store, in front of a real person, in a familiar environment. That experience is categorically more trust-building than a first solo bill payment at home.
  • A first-purchase discount at participating vendors when paying with PayWise for the first time — e.g. 5% off. PayWise subsidises this discount for the first 90 days. The consumer gets a visible, immediate benefit.

Why the Two Sides Activate Each Other

The consumer has a concrete reason to walk into a local store and use PayWise. The vendor sees a real customer paying digitally — probably for the first time. That moment is worth more than any ad in T&T's trust-driven market. It is visible, social, and local.

This mechanic addresses a specific gap in the current onboarding model: the first transaction for a consumer is currently a solo, private action (paying a bill at home). The programme makes the first transaction a social, visible, community-embedded action instead. That distinction matters enormously for the 30+ demographic, whose trust is formed through observation of people they know, not through personal experimentation.

T&T-Specific Advantages
  • Local vendors are already trusted community anchors — their implicit endorsement carries authority no marketing budget can purchase.
  • The physical sticker bypasses the "is this app real?" question. A sticker on a shop door requires nothing — it signals normalcy.
  • Keeping the wallet credit redeemable only at community vendors keeps money circulating locally — a message that resonates in Caribbean market culture.
  • The structural gap is identical to Argentina's: Apple Pay and Google Pay don't work with local cards in T&T. PayWise has the same first-mover window.
Programme Risks and Mitigations
Risk Mitigation
PayWise absorbs subsidy cost with no guaranteed return Set a hard 90-day window. After that, standard fees apply. The habit is formed or the vendor has churned — either way the subsidy ends cleanly.
Low vendor quality damages the consumer experience Set minimum eligibility criteria: established storefront, existing foot traffic, no active complaints. Quality over volume in the first cohort.
Consumer wallet credit goes unspent Make the credit visible and time-limited (expires in 30 days). A countdown nudge via WhatsApp at day 20 converts holdouts.
Insufficient vendor network at launch Seed at least 50 participating vendors in high-footfall areas (Port of Spain, San Fernando, Chaguanas) before the consumer-facing campaign goes live.
Vendors misunderstand the discount reimbursement model Train vendors at onboarding. Include a one-page FAQ in the signup pack. Provide a WhatsApp support line for vendor queries.
Implementation Priority
  1. Define vendor eligibility criteria and build the signup flow for vendors
  2. Produce physical onboarding packs (sticker, one-page guide, FAQ card)
  3. Seed 50 vendors in target areas before any consumer campaign launches
  4. Launch consumer-facing campaign with wallet credit offer
  5. Monitor redemption rate, vendor retention, and consumer repeat transaction rate at 30 and 60 days
  6. Evaluate subsidy cost vs. activated user LTV at day 90 before deciding whether to extend, graduate, or close the programme

Summary
Conclusion & The Winning Combination
The one sequence that makes all of this work — and the conditions that must be true before scaling.

The fastest path to activation is a better first-use experience that removes friction, establishes trust at every decision point, and delivers a clear bill payment success within 48 hours of signup. For lapsed users, the fastest path back is a WhatsApp message tied to a real upcoming bill — deep-linked directly to the payment screen.

The Winning Combination

Trust-first onboarding → single surfaced action → first-transaction reward → real-world bill trigger → WhatsApp-led reactivation → referral prompt after trust is formed → dual-sided vendor programme to embed the first transaction in community life.

No part of this can be scaled before the push/SMS infrastructure is live and before the onboarding flow is simplified to one first action. The PayWise Local programme additionally requires a minimum vendor network before the consumer-facing campaign launches.

The activation gap is not a marketing problem — it is an onboarding design problem with a trust layer on top. Solving it with more acquisition spend before fixing the funnel will accelerate losses, not growth.
